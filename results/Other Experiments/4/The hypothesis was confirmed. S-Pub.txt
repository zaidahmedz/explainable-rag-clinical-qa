What the experiment demonstrates, in a logical chain:
Retrieval matters enormously — every RAG system (0.53–0.65) crushes no-RAG (0.32). ~20–33 accuracy points from grounding alone. Your core thesis, confirmed.
The embedder's training objective matters more than domain vocabulary alone — BioBERT (biomedical vocab, no similarity training) is the weakest retriever (hit rate 0.880) and weakest RAG system (0.53). MiniLM (general vocab, has similarity training) jumps to 0.996 hit rate and 0.64 accuracy. So a similarity objective beats domain vocabulary when they're pitted against each other.
Combining both is best — S-PubMedBert (biomedical vocab and similarity training) edges out MiniLM on accuracy and macro-F1 (0.652 / 0.557 vs 0.640 / 0.543). The domain knowledge adds a small but real gain once the similarity objective is present.
That's a genuinely nice finding: for biomedical RAG, you want an embedder that is both domain-adapted and similarity-trained; domain adaptation alone (raw BioBERT) actually hurts.
Honest nuances to report accurately, not oversell:
The S-PubMedBert vs MiniLM gap is small — 0.652 vs 0.640 accuracy, and MiniLM actually has a marginally higher hit rate (0.996 vs 0.994) and slightly lower hallucination (0.090 vs 0.094). So the honest claim is "S-PubMedBert is best overall, but the margin over MiniLM is modest; the large, decisive gap is between both of these and BioBERT." Don't claim a dramatic domain win — claim a consistent-but-small one, which is what the data shows.
Notice S-PubMedBert also has the best "maybe" F1 (0.267) and a more balanced prediction distribution — worth a sentence.
The "maybe" class remains the universal weak point across all four systems (0.18–0.27), tied to PubMedQA's class imbalance — a dataset limitation, not a method flaw.
